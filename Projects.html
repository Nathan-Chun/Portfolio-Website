Cart-Pole Stabilization - Optimal Control, Simulation, Mechanical Design, Embedded Systems, Manufacturing, Experimental Design
Cart pole pendulum.

During my mechanical engineering studies at USC, I discovered that optimal control theory wasn't offered in the undergraduate curriculum. Seeking other avenues for further enrichment, I used online resources from leading researchers like Steve Brunton, Zach Manchester, and Russ Tedrake, to build my knowledge of the mathematical foundations of control theory, exploring everything from Karush-Kuhn-Tucker (KKT) conditions to trajectory optimization and stability analysis.

Among the multitude of concepts I encountered, the Linear Quadratic Regulator (LQR) stood out with particular elegance. Despite its linear formulation, LQR exhibits remarkable effectiveness in controlling complex systems. This counterintuitive result ignited my curiosity about the potential of LQR in real-world systems.

Inspired by mentors at the USC Dynamic Robotics and Control Lab, I set out to explore this phenomenon through the lens of the classic cart-pole system. What began as a simulation exerise to understand LQR's stabilization stabilization capabilities expanded into the more ambitious challenge of designing and controlling the physical hardware itself.

This documentation chronicles this journey from mathematical theory to working hardware, recounting both the successes and inevitable challenges that emerge at the transition between control theory and reality.

CNC Router - Mechanical Design, Electrical Circuits, Manufacturing
CNC router with custom aluminum frame and limit switches.
Inspired by my experience machining at the Baum Family Makerspace, I designed a desktop-sized CNC router capable of machining Aluminum. Using MasterCAM and DXF files, I personally programmed and operated the USC makerspace's OMAX waterjet and HAAS VF-2 mill to manufacture the parts. Limit switches were also hand soldered and implemented for added safety. Computer vision projects will be done in the near future to enable automated milling.
Analysis of Fin Separation on Convective Heat Transfer - Mathematical Analysis, Experimental Design, MATLAB, LabView
Both iterations of the custom modular heat sink base and fins on the HAAS VF-2 mill.
Heat sinks are ubiquitous in electronics, automotive systems, and other temperature critical applications. They're typically easily identifiable by their fins, which increase surface area and improve heat dissipation. This project aims to answer the question of what fin spacing maximizes heat dissipation. This optimal fin spacing was determined through a combination of mathematical analysis and experimental trials. Check out the project page for full details.
MEMS Pressure Sensor - Prototyping, Experimental Design, Electrical Circuits, NI DAQ
Fully assembled pressure sensor.
Applying concepts from the USC Introduction to Microelectromechanical Systems course (AME455), I collaborated with a team of four to create a macroscale MEMS pressure sensor. As shown in the figure above, the walls of the sensor were angled at 54.7 degrees to facilitate fabrication at the micro or nano scale. Strain gauges were adhered between two silicone rubber membranes, which were screwed down and sealed with glue. Since strain gauge/Wheatstone bridge readings were diffcult to consistently track on digital multimeters, we built a LabView virtual instrument to output voltage-time graphs. Experimental results showed a linear trend at pressures of 0.5-1.5psi.
Pressure vs Voltage plot showing an initial linear trend followed by a plateau at higher pressures. Sensor calibration factor was determined to be ~24.6mV/psi.
As shown in the figure above, the sensor isn't reliable at pressures beyond 1.5psi. Since the silicone membrane material's high elasticity wouldn't likely limit the strain gauge precision, we speculate that the strain gauges begin to slip due to poor adherance. Air leakage through imperfect seals could've also limited the sensor's readable range.
Energy Generating Shoe Soles
3D printed TPU shoe soles embedded with piezoelectric crystals wired up with makeshift lightup shoes.
Inspired by the winged shoes of Hermes, I set out with my USC Makers team to design light-up shoes powered by footsteps. After embedding piezoelectric crystals into 3D printed shoe soles, we used a bridge rectifier circuit to supply DC current to LEDs. We successfully powered 8 LEDs per shoe and presented our work at the USC Makers showcase.
