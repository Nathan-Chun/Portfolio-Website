Effective Teleoperation for Robotic Manipulation and Locomotion
Demonstration of two stage teleoperation (RRT* followed by Joystick control) and collision avoidance.
To improve workplace safety for construction workers, a we aim to develop a universal control and user framework that can be used to safely operate a variety of machines including cranes, demolition machines, and more. This framework will enable users to teleoperate construction machines safely without compromising crucial sensory cues that enable workers to precisely operate the machines. Working towards this goal, we’ve developed a Gazebo-ROS2 simulation that uses control barrier functions (CBF) to avoid obstacles and allows users to interact through VR and joystick control. The novelty of this framework is its applicability to a variety of construction machines including demolition machines, bulldozer, cranes, and more.
Gazebo simulation (left) and MeshCat obstacle visualizer (right). Lidar (blue horizontal ray plane) generates point cloud data of the cylinder. Then, an ML clustering algorithm generates a spherical barrier (shown in green) which the arm avoids using a control barrier function. Users can control the arm using a joystick without worrying about obstacle collision.
We are currently building a full obstacle course and refining the path planning method. After simulations are complete, we will deploy the framework on a real Brokk 120DII machine and perform necessary system integration.
Robot Gripper for Quadrupeds
SolidWorks rendering of the gripper on a Unitree A1 quadruped.
Robot-robot interaction is an active area of research that is complex both from a hardware and control perspective. Hierarchical control is one method to quickly solve for a optimal trajectories and account for complex dynamics in these collaborative settings. To faciliate collaborative object manipulation with quadrupeds, I designed a simple, but effective robotic gripper. Furnished with an A1 and servo motor, the arm has 2 degrees of freeedom. The paper, titled "Hierarchical Control Framework for Collision-Free Collaborative Loco-manipulation of Large and Heavy Objects," is currently under review for CASE 2025.
HECTOR v2 Low-Level Control Board
Low-Level Control board KiCad rendering. H-shape extended legs are for mounting to the walls of HECTOR's body.
The HECTOR v2 humanoid is the crown jewel of the USC Dynamic Robotics and Control Lab. My crucial role in its development was the design of the low level control board, which enables communication between the onboard computer and motors in HECTOR. Equipped with a Teensy 4.1 MCU, ethernet port, power port, 3 CAN transceivers, 5 RS485 transceivers, and an IMU, the board is analogous to the cerebellum, enabling motor control throughout HECTOR. Throughout the board's iterations accessories such as a buzzer and extra IO pins have been added. Several papers have been and continue to be published using HECTOR v2.
Wheelchair Stabilizer
Fully assembled wheelchair stabilizer with raspberry pi, Unitree A1 BLDC motor, and 24V battery.
Sudden acceleration, uneven weight distribution, and uneven surfaces can cause wheelchair users to fall out and suffer injuries. I developed a modular, motorized wheel that uses an IMU to track wheelchair angle and load cells to monitor the user's state. In the event of an imminent fall, the stabilizer quickly corrects the wheelchair position.
